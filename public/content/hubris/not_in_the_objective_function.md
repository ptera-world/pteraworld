# not in the objective function

you did the things you were supposed to do. you showed up on time, you took the shifts you were offered, you kept your head down when your head down was what the situation required. maybe you went back to school. maybe you moved somewhere with more opportunity, which cost money you didn't quite have. the calculus was reasonable. you were applying what you'd been told was the formula.

and then the rent went up again. or the hours were cut. or the position that was supposed to materialize after the probationary period quietly didn't materialize, and the person who told you it would is no longer with the company.

the question that shows up here — the one that feels like it should have a clear answer — is: did you do something wrong?

## what gets scored and what doesn't

the organization you work for isn't trying to harm you. that's worth saying clearly, because it matters. there's no malice in the mechanism. the company is trying to survive, and survival is measured in margin, and margin is improved by reducing cost, and you are a cost. not a person who is also a cost. a cost, in the part of the spreadsheet where costs live.

this doesn't require anyone to be a villain. the person making the staffing decision is probably also worried about their position. the metric they're held to doesn't include a line for "employee stability" or "cumulative toll of shift unpredictability." those things are real — they affect whether people can make rent, whether they can arrange childcare, whether they have any ground to stand on — but they're not in the model. so they don't affect the decision.

the system works. it produces the outcomes it was shaped to produce.

## what does "you can make it" actually predict?

the phrase "you can make it if you work hard" is not technically wrong. some people do make it. the ones who do are real, and the story of how they did it is usually also real. the phrase draws on genuine cases.

what it doesn't tell you is the rate. how many people applied the same effort under similar conditions and found the floor had moved. not because they failed, but because the structure they were inside was optimizing for something that didn't include them. the person who made it was in a slightly different position — started with a little more, had a network that reached the right place at the right moment, had the kind of stability that let them take a risk that paid off. the structure rewarded that. it's the structure working.

the person who didn't make it was also, in most cases, doing what the formula said. the formula just wasn't operating on a level field.

## what exhaustion does to a person

a tired person can't think as far ahead. the bandwidth that would go toward longer-term planning — toward noticing patterns, toward building something that compounds — is consumed by the immediate: this week's hours, this month's rent, whether this symptom is something that can wait until things are calmer. things don't get calmer.

this is not an accusation that exhaustion is deliberately engineered. it might be an observation about what exhaustion produces, and whether the structure has any incentive to prevent it. a person who is stretched thin does not push back. does not organize. does not have the slack to pursue anything that might take months to pay off. they do the work that's in front of them, because the work in front of them is the thing that can't be deferred.

that's a convenient outcome for a system that needs compliance and has priced out everything that would generate it voluntarily. whether it was designed that way or whether it simply selects for it doesn't change what it produces.

## what's personal and what isn't

the difficulty of separating "I failed" from "the structure failed me" is that you're inside it. from inside, they feel identical. the outcomes land on your body either way — the anxiety, the small daily indignities, the calculation of whether you can afford the thing that would make the rest of it more survivable. the system does not announce itself as the cause. it just produces outcomes. you're left to interpret them.

and the interpretation that's most available — the one that's been packaged and handed to you for the entirety of your life — is that outcomes reflect choices. that where you end up is a function of what you put in. this is partly true. it's true enough to feel true. and it routes the discomfort inward, which is useful for a structure that would rather not be named as the variable.

the version that's harder to reach — not because it requires unusual intelligence, but because you'd need time and space that the structure doesn't leave you — is that some of what happened wasn't about you at all. it was about which direction the system moves wealth, who the rules were written to protect, what gets scored as success and for whom.

you being a cost to be minimized was never personal. you were just not in the part of the model where people are considered.
