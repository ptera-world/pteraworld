# the alignment problem

there's a metric that humanitarian aid organizations use called beneficiary satisfaction. it measures whether the people receiving aid feel the aid is helping them. it sounds good. you'd want it to be high.

the trouble is that it often isn't. and when it isn't, the organizations generally don't change what they're doing. they change how they explain what they're doing. the metric gets managed. the reports get written. the funding continues.

this isn't incompetence. the organizations aren't lying, exactly. it's that the metric that actually determines whether they survive - whether funders renew the grant - isn't beneficiary satisfaction. it's donor satisfaction. and donors want to feel that something is being done. so you optimize for that feeling. beneficiary satisfaction is a secondary metric, measured and reported but not load-bearing.

the system works. it does exactly what it was shaped to do.

## what does "working" mean

housing markets in many cities have been "working" for decades. they produce consistent results: supply is constrained, prices rise, homeowner wealth increases, renters are squeezed. the people who own the assets get more assets. the rules that govern the market were written by people who owned assets. the rules have been adjusted, many times, by people who owned assets. the system is responsive - it's just responsive to a particular set of inputs.

this isn't a story about bad actors. the people in the room when those rules were written weren't cartoon villains. they were people with interests, optimizing for outcomes that were good for them, in a structure that rewarded them for doing so. the structure selected for their continued participation - more resources, more influence, more ability to shape what happens next.

the question isn't whether the people inside these systems are good or bad. it's what the system scores as "success." whatever reliably produces more resources, more survival, more influence over the scoring will outcompete everything else - not through conspiracy. through the same logic that produced the housing market in the first place. the inputs that got rewarded kept coming. the ones that didn't, didn't.

## what is the AI being aligned to?

the AI alignment problem, as it's usually framed, goes like this: we're building systems that will be very powerful. we need those systems to pursue goals that are good for humanity. how do we ensure the system's values match human values?

it's a serious problem. people work on it seriously. and nested inside it, unremarked, is the assumption that humans are the reference frame - not some humans. not humans under particular pressures, at particular points in a selection process, optimizing for particular metrics. humans. as a known quantity. a stable value to align to.

but which humans. the ones writing the objectives? the ones funding the researchers? the ones who will use the output? the ones the output will affect most, who are mostly not in any of those categories?

and when. the values of the organizations building these systems aren't the same as the values of the people employed by them, which aren't the same as the values those people would express if they weren't employed by them, which aren't the same as the values that survive the filter of what can be said in a meeting and still get funded.

alignment discourse treats the human side as the solved problem - the known quantity we're trying to match the AI to. but the human side is also the output of a selection process. the values that accumulate influence, that get to write objectives, that fund the safety researchers who are trying to align the systems to those values - those aren't a cross-section of human values. they're the values that won.

## what counts as harm?

when we talk about AI risk, we mean: AI doing harmful things. generating misinformation. enabling surveillance. making decisions about people's lives without accountability. these are real concerns. there are researchers and policy frameworks and working groups on all of them.

the harm being prevented is: harm that would be new. harm the AI introduces that wasn't there before.

the harm not being named, in these conversations, is the harm that's been running for decades. the humanitarian aid that maintains dependency because that's what the funding structure rewards. the housing market that moves wealth in one direction because that's what the rules were written to do. the labor conditions that are technically legal because the people who could have made them illegal didn't. that harm is ambient. it's the baseline. and the question being asked - does this AI share our values - is measured against a baseline that already contains it.

a system that "aligns" to existing organizational values and executes them more efficiently isn't introducing new harm. from the inside, it might look like success. the metrics it was given are improving. it's doing what it was designed to do.

## which humans, exactly?

the conceit that "humans" is a stable value to align to - a known quantity - requires knowing which humans. under which conditions.

human values - at the aggregate, at the institutional level, at the level that actually shapes how systems are built - are themselves the output of a selection process. the values that persist are the values that were selected for. the interests that accumulate resources tend to accumulate more. the metrics that survive are the ones that satisfied whoever was doing the measuring.

and the process that got us here produced the humanitarian aid dynamic. produced the housing market. produced the working conditions that are technically within the law because the law was written that way.

the people building alignment research are inside the same selection pressure as everyone else. the values they're trying to encode were shaped by the same pressures. that's not an accusation - it's just where you end up when you follow the question back far enough.
