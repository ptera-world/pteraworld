# the system is working

## what does it mean for a system to work?

it depends on who you ask, and more importantly, it depends on who gets to define "working."

the housing crisis is a useful example. millions of people can't afford to live near jobs, near family, near the city where they grew up. from one angle, this looks like failure — a system that was supposed to house people, not housing them. from another angle, it looks like success: asset values have compounded steadily for decades, the people who hold those assets have accumulated influence proportional to that wealth, and the rules governing housing continue to reflect their priorities.

both descriptions are accurate. they're just describing different fitness functions.

## does a system need bad actors to produce bad outcomes?

not really, and this is where it gets interesting.

you don't need anyone to be malicious for greed to become the dominant strategy. you just need a fitness function that rewards it — and time. over enough generations, the people and organizations who pursue resources most aggressively end up with more resources, more access to the rooms where rules get made, more ability to shape what counts as success next time. the greedy didn't corrupt the system. the system ran a tournament, and they won it.

the people at the top of that tournament aren't villains in any meaningful sense. they responded rationally to the incentives in front of them. so did everyone else. the difference is that their rationality compounded.

## what does reform look like from inside this?

a lot of reform effort is spent on the exhaust rather than the engine. cleaner exhaust is worth something — real harm reduction happens that way. but if the underlying fitness function stays intact, the system keeps producing the same outputs. you can regulate the worst individual behaviors without changing what the tournament selects for.

this applies to humanitarian aid, to labor conditions, to healthcare. the organizations that survive in each sector are the ones that learned to satisfy whoever controls the funding. not because they're cynical, but because the alternative is to not survive. the metric that actually determines whether an institution persists isn't beneficiary welfare — it's funder satisfaction. and funders want evidence that something is being done. so you optimize for that evidence.

the people the system harms most don't usually control the funding. so their satisfaction isn't part of the fitness function. it's not that they've been excluded deliberately — it's that the tournament was already underway before they arrived.

## and the explanations?

the same system that produces the housing crisis also produces the economists and think tanks and op-eds that explain why the housing crisis is necessary. this isn't conspiracy. it's just that the people with resources to fund explanations are the people the current arrangement is working for. they're not suppressing other explanations — they're just funding the ones that make sense from where they're standing.

you're not being lied to, exactly. you're receiving the conclusions that survived.

## what would it mean to change it?

you'd have to touch the fitness function — change who gets to define success, who controls the metric, who has enough resources to remain in the tournament long enough for their priorities to compound. that's a different kind of intervention than optimizing the existing process. it's slower, less measurable, and it produces no clean wins to point at.

which is also a fitness function problem. the interventions that are easiest to fund are the ones that produce legible short-term results. so the system, here too, selects for the interventions least likely to challenge it.

that's not a reason to stop. it's just useful to know what you're working against.
