# the score becomes the thing

a painting has no price until someone assigns one. before the auction, it's paint on a surface - whatever it does to you when you stand in front of it, that's between you and it. then the hammer falls, and afterward the price is its primary fact. not one fact among many. the one that travels. the one that appears in the headline, in the insurance document, in the conversation about whether it's worth seeing.

the painting didn't change. what changed is that it became readable to a system that needed it to be readable.

## what does capital actually want?

the obvious answer is money. but the billionaire who keeps going past any point of personal comfort isn't accumulating money to spend - they have more than could be spent. what they're accumulating is score. the number that goes up. and the number going up is the point, not the prelude to something else.

this matters because it means capital isn't trying to acquire things. it's trying to measure itself. and to measure itself, it needs things to have prices. it needs the world to be countable - something that can be added up, compared, reported. this isn't a preference. it's baked into the logic. you can't keep score on what you can't count.

## what gets funded?

research that produces patents. publications in ranked journals. clinical trials with clear endpoints. the things that generate a measurable output - something you can point at, something that can be cited, something that counts in an evaluation - get institutional support. the things that resist that form, the inquiry that takes a decade to become anything, the questions whose answers don't have a natural unit, those have to fight harder for the same table.

universities didn't decide to defund the humanities. the humanities just couldn't produce the kind of evidence that survives a budget meeting.

## what about the things that can't be scored?

care work is the clearest example. raising a child, sitting with someone who is sick, holding the emotional load of a family or a team or a friendship - this is some of the most consequential work that happens. the people who do it bear the weight of it in their bodies. and the system has almost no way to value it, because there's no output that can be inspected and priced.

so it doesn't get priced. it gets called love, or duty, or what you do for family, which is a way of saying: we've decided this doesn't count on the scorecard. and then the scorecard is used to make decisions about whose time is scarce and whose is available, who gets paid and who provides, what kinds of work deserve support and what kinds are just expected.

the score isn't neutral. it's not a measurement of what matters - it's a measurement of what can be measured, gradually becoming indistinguishable from what matters.

## how does culture absorb this?

not through any decision. through selection.

the relationship that can be quantified - follower counts, engagement metrics, mutual network value - gets platform support. algorithms are designed around it, because the platform needs to optimize for something, and something quantifiable is what's available. the relationship that can't be quantified doesn't get that. you can still have it. it just won't be amplified, surfaced, supported by infrastructure.

over time, the things that get amplified are the things that can be scored. the things that can be scored are the things that get developed, refined, made easier to do. the others don't disappear - they just get smaller. less resourced. harder to sustain against the things that have the infrastructure behind them.

this is the cascade: capital needs a score → the score requires everything to be countable → culture reorganizes around what can be counted → everything that resists scoring gradually loses the institutional support that would let it thrive.

## did anyone choose this?

you can't find the meeting where the decision was made. there was no meeting. there were a lot of smaller decisions - what to fund, what to measure, what to count as success in an evaluation - each of which made sense from where the decision-maker was standing. the organism that survived those decisions is the one we have now.

the parts of culture that persisted are the parts that could justify themselves on the scorecard. the parts that couldn't are mostly still here, but smaller, and staffed by people who are subsidizing them with time they could otherwise spend elsewhere.

what you end up with is a culture that didn't choose this - a culture that was selected into it, the way a species is selected by an environment it didn't design. and the environment keeps selecting. not through malice. through the quiet logic of what gets renewed.
