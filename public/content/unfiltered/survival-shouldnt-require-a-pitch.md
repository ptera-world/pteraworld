# Survival shouldn't require a pitch.

someone needs surgery. insurance denied it, or [the benefit ran out](/unfiltered/the-system-monetized-the-damage), or there is no insurance. the system's answer is: make a GoFundMe.

write your story. pick a good photo. set a goal. share it. hope the algorithm likes you. hope your network is big enough. hope your suffering is relatable enough. hope you go viral.

kickstarter for people's lives.

## how it works

crowdfunding was supposed to be for creative projects. indie films, albums, gadgets, community stuff. then healthcare happened to it.

now a significant percentage of GoFundMe campaigns are medical. not emergencies in the disaster sense - emergencies in the "the system doesn't cover this and I'll die without it" sense. routine healthcare failures dressed up as personal tragedies.

and the platform works the same way for a cancer patient as it does for an indie game. you need a pitch. you need a narrative. you need visuals. you need reach. you need shares. you need the algorithm to surface you. you need people to feel something when they see your page.

survival depends on storytelling skill.

## who gets funded

not the most desperate. the most visible.

getting funded requires digital access, platform literacy, a social network, writing skills, emotional framing ability, audience reach, time and energy to campaign, and the cultural capital to perform need in a way that resonates.

the people you see on GoFundMe have all of that. they're not the most vulnerable - they're the most platform-capable of the vulnerable. the visible poor. the narratable sick.

and even among them, the ones who get funded are the ones with a story interesting enough for strangers to care about. not just sick - *compellingly* sick. not just in need - in need in a way that makes a good post. a kid with a rare disease. a young parent. a story with a hook. the person with the same common chronic condition as a million other people, needing the same medication they can't afford - that's not a story. that's a statistic. statistics don't go viral.

so the system selects twice. first for who can ask. then for whose asking is interesting enough to fund. need doesn't win. narrative does.

behind every funded campaign are people who can't write, can't navigate the platform, don't have a social network to share with, don't speak the dominant language, don't have the cognitive bandwidth to market their own suffering. they don't get campaigns. they don't get donations. they don't get visibility.

they just disappear.

[the silence](/unfiltered/expression-has-a-cost-floor) talks about how expression has a floor - a minimum cost. GoFundMe proves it. even asking for help requires resources the most desperate don't have. the floor is there too.

## what it does to the problem

GoFundMe does something structurally dark: it reframes systemic failure as personal tragedy.

instead of "the healthcare system is broken," it becomes "this family needs help." one campaign at a time, one story at a time, one share at a time. each one individualizes the problem. each one privatizes the responsibility. each one says: here's a person in crisis - donate. not: here's a system that produces crisis - change it.

charity replaces rights. care becomes content. dignity becomes narrative. need becomes performance. survival becomes virality.

the compassion is real - people giving to people, strangers helping strangers. that part is beautiful. and the structure it exists inside is horrifying. because people *have to*. because the system that should catch these people didn't. the GoFundMe isn't supplementing the safety net. it *is* the safety net. and it has the selection bias of a social media algorithm.

## the progress bar

there's a progress bar on every campaign. a number and a goal. $12,847 of $50,000. a percentage. green filling up.

someone's life, represented as a loading screen.

and when it doesn't fill - when the campaign stalls at 23%, when the shares stop, when the algorithm moves on - the person is still sick. the surgery is still needed. the number just stopped climbing. the platform moved on to the next story. the attention economy doesn't do follow-up.

the progress bar is the most honest UI element in the entire system. it tells you exactly what's happening: this person's survival is being metered. incrementally. publicly. one donation at a time. and whether it completes is a function of reach, not need.

## what this has to do with everything else

[the benefit](/unfiltered/the-system-monetized-the-damage) talks about healthcare as a line item - budgeted, capped, tied to employment. the campaign is what happens after the benefit fails. first the system caps your treatment. then it sends you to the crowd. first it limits how much you're worth fixing. then it tells you to pitch strangers for the rest.

[the rules](/unfiltered/the-rules-were-bought) explains why: the industries that profit from this structure lobby to keep it. the complexity is the business model. the dysfunction is profitable for someone. and the people living inside the dysfunction are too busy campaigning for their own survival to change the rules that created the need.

[the floor](/unfiltered/nobody-told-you-there-were-prerequisites) says the people most affected are the least likely to be reading, writing, making anything at all. the campaign proves it at scale: even the act of asking for help - the bare minimum of participation - requires resources the most desperate don't have.

the system didn't just fail to provide care. it turned the failure into a platform. gave it a UI. added a progress bar. and called it community.
