# The system monetized the damage.

your employer offers mental health coverage. therapy sessions, maybe an EAP hotline, maybe a meditation app subscription. it's listed in the benefits package between dental and the 401k match.

this means: the system acknowledged that work causes psychological damage, and rather than fix the work, it made the treatment part of the compensation. "we know this will hurt you. here's a copay."

## how the loop works

the job causes stress. the stress causes burnout. the burnout shows up in your performance review. the performance review causes more stress. the company offers therapy as a benefit. the therapy helps you cope with the job. you cope well enough to keep working. the cycle continues.

nobody in this loop is fixing the thing that's broken. the therapy isn't there to make you well. it's there to make you functional. functional enough to keep producing. the goal isn't health. the goal is output with fewer interruptions.

and if you actually burn out — if the coping stops working and the performance drops and the reviews get bad — that's a you problem. you had access to the resources. you had the EAP number. you had the meditation app. the system gave you everything it was supposed to give you. if you still broke, that's on you.

## the bundle

the healthcare is tied to the job. in most of the US, losing your job means losing your coverage. the thing that was breaking you was also the thing paying to manage the breaking.

think about what that means. you can't leave the thing that's hurting you without also losing access to the thing that's treating the hurt. the exit costs include losing the treatment for the damage the thing you're exiting caused. that's not a safety net. that's a lock.

and everyone knows this. every person who's stayed in a job that was grinding them down because they couldn't afford to lose the insurance — they know. it's not a secret. it's not a conspiracy. it's just the structure, sitting there, doing exactly what it was designed to do.

## what burnout actually costs you

burnout doesn't show up as "burnout" in a performance review. it shows up as: missed deadlines. lack of initiative. reduced output. poor collaboration. not a team player.

the system doesn't have a category for "this person is depleted because the work depleted them." it has categories for: meets expectations, exceeds expectations, does not meet expectations. and burnout puts you in the third one. which means: worse review, smaller raise, fewer opportunities, more pressure to perform, more burnout.

the person who's drowning gets told to swim harder. and the feedback is delivered using the same professional language as everything else, so it doesn't even register as cruelty. it registers as objectivity. "the data shows your output dropped." yes. it did. the data doesn't show why.

## mental health as a line item

dental is a benefit. vision is a benefit. mental health is a benefit. a line item in the compensation package. something the company provides because it's part of the market rate for talent.

not a basic necessity. not a thing every person needs access to regardless of employment status. a perk. something your employer gives you. something you negotiate for. something that varies by plan tier and network and whether your therapist takes your insurance and whether you've hit your deductible.

the framing tells you everything. when something is a "benefit," it's a gift from the employer. when something is a right, it exists whether or not you have an employer. making healthcare a benefit means making employment the prerequisite for treatment. which means making employment non-optional. which means the negotiation between you and your employer isn't really a negotiation. they have something you can't live without, and the something isn't the salary.

## the quiet part

most people don't talk about this at work. not because they don't see it. because talking about it is a career risk.

saying "this job is burning me out" in a performance review is not a neutral statement. it's a signal. it says: this person might leave. this person might underperform. this person is a liability. the honest version — "the structure of this work is causing psychological damage" — gets translated by the system into "this person can't handle it."

so you don't say it. you use the EAP. you see the therapist. you learn coping strategies. you come back on monday and perform the version of yourself that meets expectations. the therapy helped — not by making you well, but by making you quieter about not being well.

## the cap

and the benefit is limited.

a certain number of therapy sessions per year. a dollar amount. a maximum. the system looked at psychological damage and decided: this much repair, and no more.

the cap is maybe the most honest part of the whole structure. it tells you exactly how much the system thinks you're worth fixing. not "until you're well." not "until the damage is addressed." until the budget runs out. the damage can keep going. the treatment can't.

and the damage doesn't know about the cap. burnout doesn't check whether you've hit your session limit. anxiety doesn't wait for the new plan year. the thing that's breaking you operates on its own schedule. the thing that's supposed to help you operates on a fiscal one.

so you learn to ration. you learn to save sessions for when it gets bad enough. you learn that "bad" has tiers — there's regular bad, which you just sit with, and bad enough to use one of your limited sessions on. you become the triage nurse for your own psychological damage, deciding which wounds are worth spending a session on and which ones you can just carry.

"i stopped going to therapy because the benefits don't cover that much."

read that sentence again. someone recognized they needed help. found a therapist. started the work — and starting is the hardest part, the part where you admit something is wrong, the part the system should be desperate to protect. and then the spreadsheet said stop. not the therapist. not the patient. the spreadsheet.

the person is still damaged. the work is still causing the damage. the treatment was working. and it ended because a number on a plan document said that's all you get.

this is what it looks like when health is a line item. the budget doesn't flex for the person. the person flexes for the budget.

## what this has to do with everything else

[the grind](/unfiltered/the-system-filled-every-gap-you-had) talks about how the system fills every hour. this is the part where the system also fills the response to the filling. you're exhausted? here's a benefit. you're burning out? here's a resource. you're drowning? here's a number you can call between 9 and 5, monday through friday, excluding holidays.

the system doesn't prevent the damage. it monetizes the repair. and it ties the repair to the thing causing the damage. and it calls this a benefit. and enough people accept the framing that it holds.

it holds because the alternative — saying "this is violent and someone is responsible and it's not abstract" — doesn't fit in a performance review. doesn't fit in a benefits negotiation. doesn't fit in the professional voice that the professional world requires.

it fits here.
