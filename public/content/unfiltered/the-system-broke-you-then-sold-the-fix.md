# The system broke you then sold the fix.

[the grind](/unfiltered/the-system-filled-every-gap-you-had) talks about what happens when the tank is empty. you scroll. you collapse on the couch. you do the thing that requires the least activation energy and gives the most immediate relief.

this essay is about what that thing is. and who's selling it to you.

## what coping looks like

it looks like the drink after work. the takeout because cooking requires energy you already spent. the impulse buy that gives you three seconds of feeling like you chose something. the binge - the show, the scroll, the snack - that fills the gap between getting home and going to sleep.

none of these are failures of willpower. they're the market's answer to mass depletion. you have eleven minutes and no cognitive resources left. the options that require nothing - the scroll, the drink, the delivery app - are right there. the options that require something - the walk, the conversation, the creative project - need a body and mind that already gave everything to [the job](/unfiltered/your-best-hours-belong-to-someone-else).

the system created the exhaustion. the market saw the exhaustion and built an economy around it. fast food, alcohol, streaming, social media, gambling, online shopping. each one is optimized for the exact state the grind leaves you in: too tired to do anything that asks something of you, too wired to sleep, looking for relief that arrives instantly and costs just enough that you don't notice until the statement comes.

## the judgment

and then the system judges you for it.

you drink too much. you eat too much. you spend too much. you scroll too much. you're not exercising. you're not cooking. you're not reading. you're not present. the coping mechanisms the market built for your exact state of depletion become evidence of your personal failure.

the health industry measures the damage - the weight, the liver, the sleep, the anxiety - and prescribes changes that require exactly the resources the grind already took. eat better. exercise. reduce stress. get more sleep. practice mindfulness. each one is correct. each one assumes a person who has time, energy, and bandwidth that [the grind](/unfiltered/the-system-filled-every-gap-you-had) already consumed.

the gap between the advice and the capacity to follow it is the same gap [just](/unfiltered/just) talks about. "just cook at home." "just go for a walk." "just put your phone down." the word erases the distance between where you are and where the advice assumes you are.

## self-care

the system's most recent move: taking coping and rebranding it as self-care.

self-care used to mean something. it meant recognizing your needs and attending to them. it meant: the system won't take care of you, so you need to take care of yourself.

then the market got hold of it. self-care became a product category. bath bombs, scented candles, meditation apps, wellness retreats, journals with prompts, subscription boxes. the system broke you and then sold you a branded recovery kit. the recovery doesn't address anything the system did. it addresses how the damage *feels*. it makes the damage more comfortable. comfortable enough to go back to work on monday.

and the framing is beautiful, in a horrifying way: *you deserve this.* you worked hard. treat yourself. the reward for surviving the grind is a purchase that makes the grind survivable for another week. the cycle is: deplete, cope, purchase, deplete. the money flows from you to the system through both the depletion and the relief.

## the hierarchy

not all coping is equal. and the system sorts it.

coping that costs money and looks aspirational - the gym membership, the yoga class, the organic meal prep, the therapy session - is "healthy." coping that costs less and looks rough - the beer, the fast food, the cigarette, the scroll - is "unhealthy." the difference between the two is mostly aesthetics and price point. both are responses to the same depletion. but one gets you praised and the other gets you judged. either way, [the damage lands in your body](/unfiltered/the-damage-lands-in-your-body).

the person with the gym membership and the meal prep isn't less depleted. they just had enough left over - money, time, activation energy - to cope in the way the system approves of. the person who ate takeout and watched four hours of television isn't lazier. they had less left over.

the hierarchy of coping is a class marker dressed up as a health choice.

the cycle is damage → cope → judge → shame → more damage → more coping. the shame is the part that locks it in. you know the drinking isn't helping. you know the scrolling isn't rest. you know the spending is making [the gate](/unfiltered/every-freedom-is-a-money-problem) harder to reach. and knowing this, while being unable to change it because the conditions that drive it haven't changed, produces shame. and shame is a feeling you need to cope with. so you reach for the thing that helps. which produces more shame.

the system doesn't need to enforce this cycle. the cycle is self-sustaining. all the system needs to do is maintain the depletion. the market handles the rest.

## what healing would require

healing isn't better coping. it's not a better app, a better habit, a better morning routine. healing would require the thing that caused the damage to stop causing the damage.

it would require [the grind](/unfiltered/the-system-filled-every-gap-you-had) to leave something in the tank. it would require [the job](/unfiltered/your-best-hours-belong-to-someone-else) to take fewer hours. it would require [the gate](/unfiltered/every-freedom-is-a-money-problem) to not require every dollar you earn. it would require conditions that make the walk, the conversation, the creative project accessible - not aspirational.

the system only funds coping. because coping keeps you functional inside the system. healing would change your relationship to the system. and that's not in [anyone's budget](/unfiltered/the-system-monetized-the-damage).
