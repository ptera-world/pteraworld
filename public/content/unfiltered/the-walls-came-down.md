# The walls came down.

[the room](/unfiltered/safe-spaces-need-everything-to-go-right) says safe spaces need everything to go right. safety is set by the least safe person present. one person who can't hold what you share — not cruel, just carrying their own shelf — and the room fails.

that was always true. but the room used to be small.

## the room used to have walls

your village. your street. your bar. the thirty people you saw every week. not because anyone chose that number — because distance was real. you couldn't be in a room with someone a thousand miles away. physics did the filtering.

and in a room of thirty people, safety was possible. not guaranteed — small rooms fail too, families fail, friend groups fail. [the room](/unfiltered/safe-spaces-need-everything-to-go-right) covers all the ways. but the odds that every person in the room could hold what you shared were nonzero. you could read the room. you could know who was in it. you could learn, over time, who could hold what. you could choose when to take something off [the shelf](/unfiltered/the-parts-of-yourself-you-put-away).

the internet removed the walls. the room became the world.

## the math

if safety requires every person in the room to hold what you share, and the room has eight billion people in it, the probability of safety is zero. not approximately zero. zero.

it doesn't matter how good most people are. the room is too big for "most" to matter. one person is enough to make it unsafe, and a room of eight billion always contains that person. always. the larger the room, the more certain the failure.

this isn't a design flaw. it's what happens when you remove walls. walls were doing the work of safety and nobody noticed until they were gone. we called the internet "connection" and didn't realize that connection without boundaries is exposure.

## then it got worse

the platforms didn't just put you in a room with strangers. they put every version of you in the same room.

your work self. your family self. the you that's funny with college friends at 1am. the you that's careful around the uncle who makes everything political. the you that cried in the car after the diagnosis. all of them, same room. same feed. same audience.

this isn't the "audience problem" — it's not that strangers are watching. it's that the platform collapsed the distance between your own contexts. you used to be different people in different rooms, and that wasn't dishonest. it was how humans have always worked. the bar and the office and the dinner table each got a version of you, and none of those versions were fake. they were all you. just different rooms, different walls, different rules about what was safe to show.

now there's one room. and the only version of you that's safe for every audience simultaneously is the flattest one. "good." the version that reveals nothing. the version that offends no one because there's nothing there to offend.

nobody took your contexts away on purpose. the platform just made one room and called it your profile, and the contexts collapsed under the weight of being visible to everyone at once.

## what walls gave you

it's easy to think of walls as limitation. the small town you couldn't wait to leave. the bubble. the echo chamber.

walls gave you a room small enough to be known in.

a room where you could see who was there. where you could learn, over twenty interactions, who could hold what. where you could make a mistake and the mistake stayed in the room instead of being screenshotted and shown to every context you've ever existed in. walls gave you edges — the ability to know where the room ended, who was inside it, what the rules were.

and walls gave you the freedom to be different in different rooms. to be loud here and quiet there. to try something on. to take something off the shelf in the one place where it might be held, instead of in a room where it definitely won't be.

the village wasn't connected. but the village was *known*. the internet is connected. but the internet is not known and cannot be. the room is too big.

## what used to happen inside walls

there's a thing that happened in egypt for three thousand years. the nile flooded every year. the agriculture was almost automatic — the river did the work. predictable surplus, millennia running. the closest thing to post-scarcity the ancient world produced.

and what they did with the surplus wasn't individual comfort. they built pyramids. temples. monuments. millions of people coordinating across generations to build things none of them would live to see finished, because they believed in the same thing and lived in the same bounded space and the surplus went into shared meaning instead of private consumption.

we have more surplus now than egypt could have imagined. it goes into feeds. into content. into things you consume alone on a couch while the person next to you consumes something different on their phone.

same surplus. different walls. egypt's surplus was shared because the space was shared. ours is private because the space collapsed into individual screens in an infinite room.

## the noodle bar

there's a reason the cyberpunk genre keeps coming back to small spaces. the bar. the ramen stall. the ripperdoc's back room. in a world where power is so ambient it's physics — megacorps, surveillance, neon, no outside — the places that matter are the ones with walls.

nobody goes to the noodle bar to connect. they go because they're hungry. and the person on the next stool is also just hungry. and the room is small enough — four stools, a counter, steam — that sometimes something happens. not engineered. not optimized. not mediated by a platform. just — the room was the right size and the stakes were low enough and nobody was performing for an audience.

[connection happens between two people](/prose/why-are-you-lonely). not in groups, not on platforms, not performed for anyone else. and the conditions for it are simple: bounded space, low stakes, no audience, enough time for the scripts to run out. the noodle bar has all four. the internet has none.

## the wall is the product

not the room. not the connection. not the safety. the wall.

the thing that bounds a space. the thing that keeps the room small enough to be real in. the thing that gives you edges — so you can know who's here, who isn't, what's safe, what the room can hold.

every platform that removes walls to maximize reach is removing the conditions for safety. every feature that shows your content to people you didn't choose is collapsing your contexts. every design decision that prioritizes growth over boundedness is making the room bigger. and bigger rooms are less safe. and less safe rooms mean more [shelves](/unfiltered/the-parts-of-yourself-you-put-away), more "good," more loneliness in a room full of everyone.

discord works because it has walls. thirty people in a server. you know who's there. nobody optimized for it. it's a place, not a feed.

the tool the world is missing isn't a better feed or a smarter algorithm or a more engaging platform. it's a wall. small spaces, owned by someone, bounded, with edges you can see. the room doesn't need to be safe. it just needs to be small enough that safety is possible again.
