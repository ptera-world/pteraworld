# AI isn't where this started

The complaint is right.

Deepfakes target real people without consent. Artists who spent years building a style find it replicated at scale without credit or payment. Markets that took communities a decade to build — illustration commissions, stock photography, voice work — get flooded in months. Not because one person got much better at the thing, but because one person with an API key can now produce the volume of a hundred. The parallelizability is the actual disruption. The harm isn't that the outputs are bad. It's that the outputs are good enough, and now there are so many of them that the signal collapses.

So: AI is bad, and that's right. Now try to find the thing underneath all the specific complaints.

## what are all these complaints about?

Run them side by side. Deepfakes: you can't tell if this video represents something that happened. AI-generated art flooding commission markets: you can't tell if this portfolio represents someone's developed practice. AI slop in search results: you can't tell if this answer represents someone who thought about the question. AI-written academic papers: you can't tell if this argument represents a student engaging with ideas.

The specific harms are different. The structure is the same: output used to be a reliable signal of something. Now it isn't.

What it used to signal was intent. Someone made this, which meant someone chose this, which meant you could read the choices and find the person. The output was evidence of a process. That process had stakes — time, effort, skill, care — and those stakes were visible in the result. You could trust the signal because faking it was expensive.

The AI complaint, underneath everything, is that the signal got cheap. Not wrong, exactly — the output can still be good. Just unreliable as evidence of the thing it used to evidence.

## did the algorithm already do this?

Before the AI wave, there was a different flood.

Search engines created a market for content that looked like answers without being answers. SEO content farms published tens of thousands of articles optimized for ranking, not for helping anyone. Sponsored content was formatted to look like editorial. Engagement-optimized feeds surfaced content that performed, not content that someone reached for. The recommendation algorithm made choices about what you'd see, and those choices looked, from the outside, like curation — like someone thought you'd like this — but there was no someone. There was a function that learned what kept you scrolling.

The complaint about algorithms was the same complaint, one frame smaller. You couldn't tell if this content was here because it was good or because it was optimized to look good enough to click. Output used to signal intent. Now optimization functions were producing output that looked intentful without anyone intending it.

The AI criticism lands harder because the volume is higher and the quality floor rose. But the structure — output that simulates the surface of intent without the substance — was already there.

## was curation ever clean?

Before the algorithm, there was the editor, the programmer, the tastemaker.

Radio stations played what labels paid to promote. Publishers chose what got printed, and their choices reflected commercial pressures they didn't disclose. Newspaper editors decided what was news, with interests that weren't always visible. The books that got reviewed, the films that got distributed, the records that got stocked — all of it ran through people whose choices looked like judgment but were also business.

This isn't a revelation. The machinery was always there. The point is that it was *invisible by default*. You heard a song on the radio and read it as: someone played this because it's good. The chain of decisions that put it there — label deals, payola, format requirements, playlist strategy — wasn't part of the surface.

There was a brief window when that changed. Webrings and blogrolls made curation *legible*. A blogroll was literally a list of other sites this person reads, which meant the curation was personal, sourced, and traceable. You could follow the chain. You could find the person behind the recommendation and understand their taste. The signal wasn't just "this is good" — it was "this person, whose other recommendations you can see, thought this was worth linking."

That's a different thing. Not just curation but *accountable* curation. The recommender was visible and had skin in it. Their reputation attached to the list.

Platforms didn't build that. They couldn't — accountability is friction, and friction is what optimization removes. The blogroll died with the personal web. What replaced it was the algorithm.

## so what did AI break?

Maybe nothing new — maybe the last remaining piece of something already mostly gone. The algorithm broke the link between curation and intent. AI broke the link between production and intent. Together they cover most of the surface of what you encounter online: the content that reaches you, and the content that exists to be reached. Both ends of the chain now produce things that look like the product of someone caring, without requiring that anyone cared.

The complaint about AI is right. It's also possible the complaint is aimed at the latest instance of a pattern that's been running for thirty years, and that pattern is what actually needs examining — what it means when output is no longer reliable evidence of the process that produced it, and whether it was ever as reliable as we thought.

The signal was always noisy. It got noisier. Then it got cheap to fake at scale. Then it got cheap to generate from scratch.

Each step felt like a new problem. It might be the same problem, getting harder to ignore.
