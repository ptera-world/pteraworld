# The synthetic plosive

When an AI voice says "play" or "person" or "please," it pops its plosives. That small burst of air pressure on the consonant — the thing that makes podcasters buy foam windscreens and wrap socks around their microphones. You've heard it. It sounds like someone who is bad at audio.

Except there's no air. No lungs, no vocal cords, no microphone catching the pressure. The pop is added in software, deliberately, because synthesized voice without it sounds too clean. A little too not-from-a-body. Somewhere, a team of engineers looked at artifact-free audio and chose to add the artifact back.

josh (with parentheses), in an essay about writing and AI, is the one who noticed this: "There is no air. There is no microphone. There is no intent." The pop is programmed. The body is simulated. And we respond to it anyway.

## what the artifact was doing

Embodiment used to be unavoidable. If sound was coming through a microphone, a body produced it — and that process left marks. Room noise. Breath sounds. The slight variation of a human throat. Plosive puffs. These weren't signals anyone was deliberately sending. They were evidence: byproducts of a process that required a body.

We learned to read those artifacts as presence. Presence as: someone was here. And someone-was-here as: someone chose this, someone meant it. The inference was almost always correct, because producing voice without a body wasn't available. So the shortcut worked. The artifact was reliable evidence of the thing it implied.

When production got cheap enough to generate voice without a body, the inference didn't update automatically. The artifact still fired it. So the artifact got designed in — not as byproduct but as signal. The pop tells the listener: this came through a body. The listener concludes: this person chose this.

## the older design move

The synthetic plosive predates AI voice synthesis.

The storefront window isn't arranged to express the owner's taste. It's arranged to produce the inference that a person with taste arranged it — the same presence-implies-intent logic, applied to objects. The recommendation algorithm doesn't surface things for you because it understands you. It surfaces them at the moment that reads as discovery rather than targeting. The "people also liked" wears the syntax of a friend's suggestion while running on a function that learned what kept you scrolling.

These are all cases where something was designed to produce the evidence of intent — to trigger the inference without the underlying process that would have earned it. The mechanism isn't new. What changed is that AI made it explicit.

When a voice synthesis team debates whether to add synthetic plosives, the question is on the table out loud: what do we add to make this sound like it came from someone? Elsewhere that question gets asked implicitly, in UI research and conversion optimization and feed algorithm design. AI is one of the first places the decision has to be named.

## what legibility costs

Once you see it in the voice, you start hearing it in other places. The feed that always seems to know. The recommendation that lands like insight. The interface designed to feel like someone made choices for you. All of them adding the pop, triggering the inference, producing the surface of someone meaning it.

The question isn't whether this is new. It isn't. The question is what changes now that the mechanism is visible — now that there's a room somewhere where engineers chose to add the artifact, and you can picture the conversation.

The inference still fires. Presence still reads as intent, even after you've seen the seam. Which might mean legibility doesn't change what happens in you — just what you can notice about what happens. Whether noticing is enough to do anything with is a different question. The collection doesn't have an answer. It just keeps pointing at the pop.
