# Someone always chose

Before the algorithm, before the feed, before the platform - someone chose what reached you.

A radio programmer decided which songs aired. A publisher decided which manuscripts became books. A newspaper editor decided what counted as news, what went above the fold, what got buried on page twelve. A record store buyer decided what got shelf space. A TV programmer decided what aired at eight o'clock on Thursday.

These were human choices, and they looked like judgment. Sometimes they were. But they also reflected label deals, advertising relationships, format requirements, personal friendships, commercial calculations, and institutional pressures that weren't part of the surface. The radio programmer played what labels promoted. The publisher chose what sold. The editor weighed newsworthiness against the interests of people who bought ad space.

This wasn't hidden, exactly. It just wasn't visible. You heard a song on the radio and the experience was: someone played this because it's good. The chain of decisions behind that moment - why this song, why this station, why this hour - wasn't available to you. You trusted the signal because you had no reason not to, and because the alternative was to curate everything yourself, which wasn't possible.

## when curation became visible

There was a window, maybe ten years, when curation became traceable.

Webrings connected sites by hand. Blogrolls were literally lists: here are the sites I read, here are the people whose thinking I follow. A personal homepage with a links page was someone showing you their taste, explicitly, with their name attached. You could follow the chain. You could find the person behind the recommendation and look at what else they recommended and decide whether you trusted their judgment.

This was different from what came before. Not just curation but *accountable* curation. The recommender had skin in it - their reputation attached to the list. If they linked you somewhere terrible, that was on them, visibly. The signal wasn't just "this is worth your time." It was "this specific person, whose other choices you can examine, thinks this is worth your time."

This happened almost by accident. The early web was personal because the tools were primitive. You built a page by hand, which meant every link was a deliberate act. The transparency wasn't a design goal. It was a side effect of the friction.

## when the feed replaced the list

Platforms solved a real problem: there was too much to curate by hand. RSS readers, social feeds, recommendation engines - they scaled discovery beyond what any blogroll could manage. And at first the tradeoff seemed fine. You lost some visibility into who was choosing, but you gained access to things you'd never have found otherwise.

The shift happened gradually. The feed started showing you things based on what you'd engaged with before, rather than what someone chose for you. The recommendation started optimizing for metrics - time spent, clicks, shares - rather than for any notion of quality or relevance that a person would recognize.

At some point the feed became an optimization function. It made choices about what reached you, and those choices looked like curation - they had the shape of "someone thought you'd like this" - but there was no someone. There was a system that learned what kept you engaged, and engagement correlated loosely with interest but more reliably with emotional activation, novelty, outrage, and compulsion.

The surface looked the same. Content appeared in front of you, as if recommended. But the process behind it had changed completely. The recommender was gone. What replaced it didn't have taste, didn't have reputation, didn't have skin in the outcome. It had a loss function.

## when production followed distribution

SEO content farms were the same move applied to the other end.

If the algorithm determined what reached people, and the algorithm rewarded certain patterns - keyword density, format, length, freshness - then you could produce content shaped to match those patterns without caring about the content itself. Articles that looked like answers without being answers. Pages that existed to rank, not to help. The surface of usefulness - headers, bullet points, confident prose - without anyone behind it trying to be useful.

This is the thing that keeps showing up. Each step in the sequence does the same thing: it reproduces the *appearance* of a human choice without requiring the human. The radio programmer's judgment gets replaced by the algorithm's optimization. The blogger's considered recommendation gets replaced by the engagement-ranked feed. The writer's attempt to be helpful gets replaced by the content farm's attempt to look helpful.

## the last piece

Generative AI completes the pattern.

The algorithm removed the human from distribution - what reaches you is optimized, not chosen. Content farms removed the human from production - what exists to rank is shaped, not made. AI does both at once, and at a scale that makes the previous versions look like rough drafts.

What's generated can be good, and that's not the issue - the issue is that the signal relationship between output and intent, the thing where seeing a piece of work meant someone cared enough to make it, is now fully severable. Output no longer requires intent, doesn't even require the *pretense* of intent, just a prompt.

Each of these transitions felt like a new problem when it arrived. Payola. SEO spam. Algorithmic manipulation. AI slop. But from a distance, they look less like separate problems and more like one process working itself out over decades: the progressive decoupling of the surface of human choice from actual human choosing.

The surface is still there. Something still reaches you. It still looks like someone thought you should see it. Whether that appearance is worth anything - whether it was ever worth as much as we assumed - is the question that keeps getting harder to avoid.
