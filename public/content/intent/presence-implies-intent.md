# Presence implies intent

When you see someone in a video, you make an inference that's so automatic it doesn't feel like an inference at all. This person chose to be here. They chose these words, this setting, this act. Their presence is evidence of their participation, and participation implies consent — which is a form of intent. They meant to do this.

This is mostly reliable. Before very recently, seeing someone in a video meant they were actually there, which meant at minimum they showed up, which meant something about what they were willing to do. The inference from presence to intent was almost always correct, because producing the appearance of someone's presence without them was technically impractical. You could misquote someone in text. You could take a photo out of context. But fabricating a video of a specific person saying specific things or doing specific things — that wasn't available, and now it is.

## what a deepfake actually does

The usual framing is misinformation: a deepfake puts false content into the world. That's true, but it's also what any fabrication does. What's specific to deepfakes is that they attach the false content to a real person's face, voice, and body. The output doesn't just say something untrue. It says something untrue *as someone*.

That's a different kind of operation. A fake news article is a lie. A deepfake is a lie that borrows someone's identity to tell itself. The subject of the deepfake didn't write those words, didn't perform that act, didn't choose to appear — but the viewer's automatic inference still fires. Presence implies intent. The video reads as: this person chose this.

The person whose face appears in a deepfake has had their intentional voice borrowed. Not their image exactly, though that's part of it. Their *agency* — the thing where seeing them do something means they decided to do it. The deepfake produces the surface of a decision that was never made.

## where the harm concentrates

The damage tracks with how much the subject's intent matters in context.

A deepfake of a public figure making a political statement is harmful because political positions are consequential. People act on what leaders say. The harm is partly that viewers might believe it, but it's also that the figure now has to respond to something they never said — their actual intent gets displaced by fabricated intent they have to spend time and credibility pushing back against.

A deepfake placing a private person in a sexual context is harmful in a different and more concentrated way. In that context, consent isn't just important — it's the entire substance. The difference between an intimate act and a violation is whether the person chose to participate. A sexual deepfake produces the visual evidence of participation without the participation. It manufactures the appearance of consent from someone who didn't give it.

These aren't the same harm, but they share a mechanism. In both cases, someone's intent — what they would choose, what they did choose — gets overwritten by output that reads as their choice but isn't.

## why "it's obviously fake" doesn't resolve it

There's an argument that deepfakes become less harmful as people learn to expect them. If everyone knows videos can be fabricated, the reasoning goes, people will stop trusting video as evidence. The problem is that the harm isn't only about belief.

Consider the sexual deepfake case. The damage to the subject doesn't depend primarily on whether viewers think it's real. The damage is that the content exists — content that wears their face, that performs their body in acts they didn't consent to, that circulates in spaces they can't reach and can't control. The content reads as their participation even if some fraction of viewers suspect it's generated. It's out there, attached to their identity, performing a version of their intent that they never held.

The same structure applies more broadly. A deepfake of someone saying something they'd never say doesn't need to convince everyone. It just needs to exist. Once it exists, the subject lives in a world where that content is attached to them — searchable, shareable, decontextualizable — and the fabricated intent sticks to the real person regardless of what anyone knows about how it was made.

## the version where simulated intent gets a name

Every instance this collection looks at involves the same basic operation: output that produces the appearance of human intent without the intent behind it. Algorithmic curation that looks like someone chose this for you. Generated text that reads like someone thought about the question. AI art that carries the surface of a developed practice.

Deepfakes are the version where the simulated intent isn't generic. It belongs to a specific, named person who didn't provide it. The output doesn't just look like *someone* intended it. It looks like *this person* intended it. That specificity is what makes the mechanism so legible here — and what makes the stakes personal in a way that other instances of the pattern aren't.

The question running through all of this is what happens when the link between output and intent breaks. With deepfakes, that question has a face attached to it. A specific person's face, doing something they never chose, in a video that reads as evidence of a choice they never made.
