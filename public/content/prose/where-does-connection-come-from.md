# Where does connection come from?

Not from wanting it. Wanting it is the thing that makes it harder, actually. The more deliberately you pursue connection the more it turns into performance, and performance is the opposite of what connection needs. You can't will yourself into being known.

So where does it come from?

## The bar stool

Think about the times you've actually connected with someone. Not "had a good conversation" — connected. Felt known, or felt like you saw them clearly, or walked away with something that wasn't there before.

Most of those times, you weren't trying. You were doing something else. Sitting next to someone at a bar. Working on the same project. Waiting for the same bus for the fifth time. Walking the same trail. The connection happened in the margins of something that wasn't about connecting.

There's a pattern: shared space, shared time, low stakes. Nobody was there *for* each other. They were there for the noodles, the work, the bus. And the connection happened because the room was small enough, and the time was open-ended enough, and nobody was performing.

[Why are you lonely?](/prose/why-are-you-lonely) talks about what seems to help — unscheduled time, no audience, repeated contact. Those conditions aren't a program you run. They're a side effect of being in the same place, regularly, doing something that isn't about each other.

## The byproduct problem

Connection might be a byproduct. Not a product. Not something you can build or engineer or schedule. Something that emerges when the conditions are right — and the conditions have almost nothing to do with connection itself.

You need a place. Bounded, small enough to recognize people. You need a reason to be there that isn't "I'm here to connect." A task, a meal, a game, a ritual, anything. You need time that isn't metered — not a one-hour appointment, not a scheduled hangout with an end time. Open-ended presence. And you need the absence of an audience — nobody watching, nobody optimizing, nobody keeping score.

Games figured this out. You join someone's world and exist in it together. The doing creates the connection. You're fighting the boss, building the base, exploring the cave — and somewhere in the middle of that, without either of you noticing the transition, you're not just playing anymore. You're *with* someone.

[What do we keep losing?](/prose/what-do-we-keep-losing) mentions this — the way games just let you be in someone's world. The rest of life made it so complicated that it's easy to forget it used to be that simple. Or maybe it was never simple. But the mechanism was: shared space, shared activity, enough time.

## Egypt built pyramids

For three thousand years, the Nile flooded every year. The agriculture was effectively automated — the river deposited the silt, the crops grew, the surplus was predictable. One of the closest things to post-scarcity the ancient world produced.

And what happened with the surplus? Millions of people coordinated to build structures that none of them would see completed. Pyramids. Temples. Monuments that took generations. Not because someone forced them — because they shared a belief, shared a space, and the surplus went into something collective.

The connection wasn't the goal. The pyramid was the goal. But you can't build a pyramid without connection. You can't coordinate a million people across decades without shared meaning. The connection was the byproduct of the shared project, and the shared project was possible because the surplus existed and someone pointed it at something communal.

We have more surplus now than Egypt had in its best century. It goes into individual screens. Not because people are selfish — because there's no shared project. No pyramid. No equivalent of "we're building this together over the next forty years." The surplus exists and it has nowhere communal to go, so it goes into private consumption, and private consumption doesn't produce connection as a byproduct. It produces comfort, alone.

## The coordination problem

The obvious question: why not just create shared spaces? Build the bar. Start the project. Make the room.

People do. Discord servers, community gardens, makerspaces, local game nights, co-working spaces. Some of them work. The ones that work tend to share the same properties: small, bounded, regular, purposeful, not optimized for growth.

The ones that don't work tend to fail for the same reason: they grow. They scale. They get popular. The room gets bigger. [Why did things stop being personal?](/prose/why-did-things-stop-being-personal) describes this — the personal web died when "good" got redefined around platforms. The small room becomes a large room because large rooms are more visible, more fundable, more legible to metrics. And large rooms don't produce connection. Large rooms produce audiences.

There's something uncomfortable here. Connection might not just need small rooms — it might need rooms that *stay* small. That resist growth. That are, by every metric the current world uses to measure success, failures. The Discord server with thirty people that never grows. The bar that doesn't franchise. The game that doesn't scale.

Staying small isn't a business model. It's barely even a choice most spaces get to make. But it might be the prerequisite.

## What the room needs

Not much. That's the hard part — it's not that the recipe is complicated. It's that the recipe is incompatible with almost every incentive the current world runs on.

A bounded space. Small enough to recognize faces. Owned by someone, or at least maintained by someone who cares about it more than they care about growing it.

A reason to show up that isn't social. The noodles, the game, the project, the craft. Something to do with your hands or your attention that takes the pressure off the connection itself.

Time. Not scheduled time with an end point. Just — being there. Long enough for the scripts to run out, for the comfortable surface to exhaust itself, for something unplanned to happen in the silence after the easy conversation ends.

And walls. Some way of knowing who's in the room and who isn't. Some boundary between inside and outside. Not to exclude — to make the inside legible. You can't know a room of eight billion people. You can know a room of thirty.

That's it. A room, a reason, time, and walls. We used to have all four without trying. The village had them by default. The church had them. The market had them. The bar had them.

We don't have them by default anymore. And building them on purpose, against the grain of every system that wants rooms to grow and walls to fall — that might be the actual work.
