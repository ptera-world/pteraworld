# This is not a personal website.

It has projects. It has essays. It has a spatial graph you can explore. It looks like a portfolio. It looks like someone presenting themselves to the world.

It's not. [Not because it's dishonest](#what-it-isnt) — because a personal website implies something [it can't deliver](#what-a-personal-website-claims). This is [one facet](#one-facet) of a person. Not the person.

## What a personal website claims

"This is me."

That's the implicit contract. You land on someone's site, you see their work, their bio, their headshot, their story. You leave feeling like you know something about them.

But what do you actually know? You know what they chose to show. Curated, arranged, presented. The professional version. The coherent version. The version that [compresses](/prose/why-are-we-compressing-things) a contradictory, messy, incomplete human into a navigable surface.

A personal website works like a [label](/prose/what-are-labels-anyway) applied to a person. "I am this." And like most labels, it tends to flatten more than it reveals.

## What it isn't

This site has no about page. No photo. No bio. No "hi, I'm X and I do Y." The essays are in second person — "you," not "I." The projects are described by what they do, not who made them.

That's not an oversight. It's a choice. The site is about ideas and artifacts, not about a person. It avoids performing personhood — because the performance would be a lie. Not a malicious lie. Just the ordinary lie that every representation tells: this is the thing, not a [projection](/prose/why-are-we-compressing-things) of the thing.

And even the artifacts aren't purely "one person's output." They came from [conversations with language models](/prose/the-great-deceit). They were shaped by collaboration that the site can't represent because the process is invisible. The reader sees essays. They don't see the rejected drafts, the 3 AM conversations, the "no" that shaped every "yes." What's visible is the residue of a process, not a portrait of a person.

## One facet

You could read every essay. Explore every project. Navigate every node. Follow every link.

And you'd still know almost nothing about the person behind it.

You'd know how they think about software, identity, change, systems. You wouldn't know what makes them laugh. What they eat. What music they listen to. Who they love. What their voice sounds like. What they look like when they're excited. What they're afraid of that isn't in any essay.

This is one facet of a [multifaceted](/prose/its-not-that-simple) person. One lens. One axis. One projection, angled to catch a specific light. Calling it a "personal website" would suggest this facet is the whole diamond.

It's not. A site *could* be closer to a person — a scratchpad, a scrapbook, messy and contradictory and unfinished. Half-thoughts next to photos next to something you were angry about at 2 AM. That would be more honest than any portfolio. But the web trained everyone to curate, and this site is [more curated than most](/prose/this-site-is-manipulative). A person is messy, contradictory, incomplete, and mostly invisible. This site is clean, coherent, interlinked, and fully visible. That mismatch is the gap that "personal website" papers over — and this site papers over it more deliberately than most.

## So what is it?

Artifacts from a process. Things that passed through a person. A graph of ideas that someone cared about enough to articulate, connected in ways that reflect how they think — [but not who they are](/prose/who-am-i).

The person [changes](/prose/everything-changes). The site stays. The person contains contradictions. The site resolves them. The person has bad days, unexpressed thoughts, undiscovered selves. The site only has what made it through the filter.

This is not a personal website. It's what's left after you subtract everything that can't be a website. Which is most of a person.
