# How do I do things?

This is a real question. It's not rhetorical and it's not a setup for advice.

There are ideas sitting in people's heads - things they want to make, learn, try. They've been there a while. Haven't started. Or started and stopped. And at some point the question stops being frustration and becomes genuine: how do people actually do things?

## Why isn't it obvious?

Doing things feels like it should be simple. Want thing, do thing. But it's not.

There's the gap between idea and execution. The uncertainty of whether it'll be good. The memory of past attempts that didn't go anywhere. The pull of easier things. The tiredness. The sense that maybe you need something you don't have - more time, more skill, more certainty, more permission.

This isn't weakness. It's just... how it is, a lot of the time.

## What seems to help

These aren't a system or productivity advice. Just patterns that seem to make it easier:

Make it small. Not "my big project" but "what's the smallest piece I could try right now?" Small enough that it doesn't matter if it's bad. Small enough that failure is cheap. Small enough to start.

Drop the identity frame. Not "am I a writer / artist / person who does things?" Just "I'm messing with this today." No declaration. No commitment. Just action without [the weight of labels](/prose/what-are-labels-anyway).

Accept that first attempts are bad. Not as a motivational slogan - actually expecting it. The first version will be rough. That's not failure; that's how it works. The goal isn't a good first attempt. The goal is a first attempt at all.

Let go of perfect. [Perfectionism](/prose/why-is-perfectionism-a-trap) looks like high standards. It's usually fear dressed up as quality control. "I can't put this out until it's good enough" becomes "I can't put this out." The standard becomes the wall. But nothing is perfect. Everything that exists is flawed. The choice isn't between perfect and imperfect - it's between imperfect and imaginary.

Notice the permission gap. Sometimes the thing in the way is waiting - for the right moment, the right conditions, someone to say it's okay. That permission might not be coming. It might already be yours.

## What if you're just lost?

Sometimes the problem isn't motivation. It's that you don't know what to do. Learning an instrument. Learning a language. Drawing. 3D modeling. Animation. The skill is big and the path is invisible.

Pick one resource. Not the best one — just one you'll actually follow all the way through. Five tutorials at once feels productive but mostly generates tab guilt. To find a decent one: look for creators who do the thing, not just teach it. Prefer community recommendations over ads. Be skeptical of anything promising fast results or selling the dream harder than teaching the skill. Rough and free often beats polished and paid.

Before you try to make something original, copy. Draw the thing someone else drew. Play the song someone else wrote. Rebuild the thing that already exists. This feels like cheating but it's how the motions get into your hands. Originality shows up on its own once you've spent enough time with the basics.

You'll be bad for longer than you want. The progress curve isn't smooth — it's flat, then jump, then flat again. Somewhere in that first long flat stretch you'll wonder if you're the exception, the person it doesn't work for. You're not. The flatness isn't failure.

If you can find someone a few steps ahead of you — not the master, someone who was where you are recently — ask them what they wish they knew. Watch how they work. Their shortcuts are more useful than the expert's because they still remember what was confusing.

You don't need to see the whole route before starting. Take one step. The next step becomes visible from there.

## Try this

- 15 minutes. That's it. Set a timer, do the thing until it goes off, stop. You're not committing to finish.
- "Work on my project" isn't an action — your body doesn't know what to do with that. "Open the file" is an action. "Write one sentence" is an action. "Pick up the pencil" is an action. Say the next physical move out loud.
- Start wherever there's energy. You don't have to begin at the beginning. The structure can come later, and usually does.
- Write the bad draft. Draw the ugly sketch. Build the broken prototype. On purpose. Lower the bar to the floor so you can step over it instead of staring at it.
- Phone in another room. Other tabs closed. Sometimes the most useful thing you can do is make the thing you want to do the easiest option available.
- Not a novel — a paragraph. Not an app — a button that does one thing. Not a song — four bars. Shrink the scope until it's almost embarrassing. Embarrassingly small is finishable.

## The only guaranteed failure

Not trying.

If you try and it doesn't work, something happened. You learned what doesn't work, or what you don't like, or what's harder than expected. That's information.

If you don't try, nothing happens. The idea stays in your head. The question stays unanswered.

There's no guarantee that trying works. But not trying has a guaranteed outcome.
