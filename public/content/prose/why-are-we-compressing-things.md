# Why are we compressing things?

Everything gets compressed. People into [labels](/prose/what-are-labels-anyway). Ideas into slogans. Experiences into metrics. Relationships into status. Software into features. Life into a [score](/prose/whats-actually-wrong).

Sometimes this is necessary. Sometimes it's [lazy](#when-is-it-lazy). Sometimes it's [harmful](#when-is-it-harmful). And it's often [hard to tell which](#can-you-tell) until it's too late.

## Do we have to?

This isn't really optional. Human brains process a finite amount of information. To navigate the world, you have to reduce, categorize, simplify. You can't treat every person as infinitely complex in every interaction. You can't hold every dimension of a problem in mind simultaneously. You can't make decisions without collapsing options.

Compression is cognition. Categories, heuristics, stereotypes, models, summaries, [labels](/prose/what-are-labels-anyway) — these are tools for making the world manageable. Without them, you'd be paralyzed by infinite detail.

So compression isn't the enemy. It's the cost of being a finite mind in an infinite world.

The question isn't whether to compress. It's [what you lose when you do](#what-gets-lost).

## What gets lost

Every compression discards information. That's what compression means. The question is whether the discarded information mattered.

**People into labels.** "She's the anxious one." Okay — but she's also funny, perceptive, loyal, creative, and anxious in specific contexts for specific reasons that the label doesn't capture. The label makes her manageable. It also makes her flat. And [she might be wearing the label too](/prose/what-are-labels-anyway), living inside a compressed version of herself.

**Ideas into slogans.** "Move fast and break things." "Be yourself." "Just do it." Each of these compresses a real insight into something repeatable and empty. The nuance was the point. The slogan lost it. And then people act on the slogan and wonder why [it doesn't work](/prose/how-do-i-do-things).

**Experience into metrics.** Mental health: 73%. Happiness index: 6.2/10. Life satisfaction: moderate. What do these numbers mean? What was lost in the measurement? The number says "moderate." The [reality](/prose/whats-actually-wrong) is a web of loneliness, purpose, fear, connection, context, and a hundred things that don't fit in a survey.

**Software into features.** This app has 200 features. That app has 150. Therefore this one is better. [That doesn't follow.](/prose/why-is-software-hard) But the compression — counting features — made it comparable. Comparable is manageable. Manageable is how decisions get made. And [quality disappears](/prose/why-is-software-hard) because it doesn't compress well.

## When is it lazy?

Sometimes compression is just the path of least resistance.

"All politicians are corrupt." That's compression. It saves you from engaging with politics. "Both sides are the same." That's compression. It saves you from making a judgment. "People don't change." That's compression. It saves you from [the hard work](/prose/why-is-change-so-hard) of updating your model of someone.

Lazy compression can feel like wisdom. It has the shape of a conclusion — definitive, settled, done. But it's more often a refusal to look more closely. The compression happens not because the detail is unavailable but because engaging with it is effort.

## When is it harmful?

Lazy compression becomes harmful when it affects other people.

Compressing a person to a label means treating them as the label. Compressing a group to a stereotype means policy based on the stereotype. Compressing a problem to a metric means solving for the metric instead of the problem.

[Life sims](/prose/whats-actually-wrong) compress human experience into meters. Happiness goes up, hunger goes down, optimize the numbers. That's not simulation — that's the compression *pretending* to be the thing it compressed. And people internalize it. "Why can't I just optimize my life?" Because your life isn't a meter. The [sim lied to you](/prose/whats-actually-wrong).

Social media compresses relationships into follower counts. Education compresses learning into grades. Work compresses contribution into performance reviews. Healthcare compresses wellbeing into symptom checklists. In every case, the compression becomes the reality — people optimize for the compressed version and lose the original.

[Goodhart's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goodhart%27s_law) is about compression: when you compress a goal into a metric, people optimize the metric and the goal disappears.

## Can you tell?

This is the tricky part: you usually can't tell when your compression is losing something important. Because the lost information is, by definition, not visible in the compressed version.

You don't notice what the label drops until someone breaks out of it and surprises you. You don't notice what the metric misses until the thing it was supposed to measure falls apart despite the numbers looking fine. You don't notice what the category erases until you meet someone who doesn't fit.

The compression hides its own losses. That's what makes it worth watching for. Not that it's wrong — it might be perfectly accurate for 90% of cases. But the 10% it gets wrong are often the cases that matter most. [The edge cases are people.](/prose/why-are-you-lonely)

## What's the alternative?

Not "never compress." That's impossible and useless advice.

The alternative is:

- **Know you're compressing.** Most of the damage happens when compression is invisible — when the label feels like a fact, the metric feels like reality, the slogan feels like wisdom. Noticing that you simplified is most of the battle.
- **Hold it loosely.** Use the compression but don't grip it. "She seems anxious" instead of "she *is* anxious." "This metric suggests" instead of "this metric proves." Leave room for the compression to be wrong.
- **Decompress when it matters.** You don't need full fidelity all the time. But when you're making a decision that affects someone, when you're [judging yourself](/prose/what-are-labels-anyway), when something important hinges on it — take the time to look past the compression.
- **Build things that resist compression.** [Software that shows complexity](/prose/why-is-software-hard) instead of hiding it. [Sims that model the mess](/prose/whats-actually-wrong) instead of flattening it. [Graphs instead of lists](/prose/its-not-that-simple). Systems that let reality be as complicated as it is.

The purpose of life is to [be yourself](/prose/whats-the-purpose-of-life). Compression is the thing that keeps turning you into a summary. The resistance is staying whole.
